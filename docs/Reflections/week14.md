# Conclusions

## What does it mean to be human in the age of AI?

To be human in the age of AI is to live in a moment of time where authenticity and the value of creativity are no longer concretely defined. AI can generate text, analyze massive datasets, and imitate creativity in ways that once seemed uniquely human. However, imitation is not the same as having experience. AI can simulate understanding, emotion, and agency, but without the systems that sustain it, nothing remains: no experience, no memory, no meaning.

AI challenges the assumption that thinking and problem-solving (sapience) define us. Machines can now perform many of these kinds of tasks faster and more efficiently than humans. But humans are not just thinking beings, we feel, interpret, and seek meaning as well. As sentient beings, emotions, relationships, memory, and personal context shape how we understand and interact with the world around us. When a person creates something, it is shaped by their experiences, culture, and values. AI, by contrast, draws from patterns in existing data. It can recombine and replicate, but it does not care about what it produces and cannot assign emotional value to its ‘creations.’ Humans embody the intersection of sapience and sentience, but AI is limited to merely imitating one half (sapience) of what makes us unique beings. Another issue of AI is not simply what it can do, but what it cannot.

Since AI lacks sentience, it subsequently lacks consciousness, emotional depth, and moral responsibility. Even when AI produces something that appears emotionally intelligent, there is often something “off” about it, such as the perfect selfies we generated. That ‘uncanny valley’ feeling highlights the gap between AI’s simulation and humans’ authenticity. Humans do not just produce outputs; we interpret them, question them, and attach significance to them. Since AI is a human-made system shaped by specific decisions, values, and biases, it is not neutral. It reflects the priorities of the people and companies that design it. This inherent bias, combined with its lack of morals and decision-making raises questions about responsibility: who is responsible when AI’s outputs reinforce inequality, spread misinformation, and contribute to environmental harm? On the other hand, AI can help boost fields like education, medicine, and creative work. I believe it all comes down to the user. AI functions on human direction and input, so the responsibility falls on users’ shoulders to use AI in an ethical way for the greater good of all.

All in all, AI forces us to ask deeper questions: What do we value about human life? What aspects of the human experience do we want to preserve? Efficiency and convenience are powerful, but they are not the same as authenticity, empathy, or justice. If we prioritize speed and automation above all else, we risk losing sight of the qualities that make life meaningful. At the same time, AI can expand human potential. It can serve as a tool that enables new forms of creativity and problem-solving. For a brighter future, we must use AI as support rather than replacement, ensuring that human intention and judgment stay central. As time goes on, we must keep an open, inquisitive mind when regarding AI. Always look further than what a company or individual wants you to see. Consider the silent workforce behind every sleek interface. Take action, educate yourself, and be able to adapt your definition of 'being human' as the world changes.
