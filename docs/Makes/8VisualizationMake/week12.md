## Research Documentation
For sources, I got my base information from both the Atlas of AI and the MIT Technology Review article. From there, I decided how to implement my visualization: the unit of measurement would be in GPT Trainings (552t CO2) so that it would be easy for the viewer to see the connections between everyday items/actions and the overall impact of AI. Then, I used the Machine Learning and Environmental calculators (as well as a regular calculator) to get the values I needed and average out outliers to provide the most well-rounded information.

Honestly, most of the information was publicly available and fairly easy to find. However, the information was also extremely vague or incredibly niche. Trying to search for exact numbers always turned up wide ranges (which I usually took the average of). Other searches were oddly specific, such as comparing a flight’s emission to electric scooter emissions…which isn’t easily visualized and not exactly useful. When deciding what to compare to the emissions from 1 ChatGPT training, I made sure to cover multiple bases: lifespan, transportation, construction, household, AI use, and carbon offset. Additionally, I wanted to include something that might surprise the viewer: the impact of their diet. Many people don’t realize the amount of carbon emissions that arise from livestock consumption, so I wanted to include the comparison to bring more awareness to it.

Other than vague/averaged data, uncertainties lie in the ‘before’ and ‘after’ stages of all of the data I found and visualized. The carbon emissions I found for each of these categories was from a single instance (units of measurement differing for each) of use, but didn’t account for manufacturing and disposal emissions. Things such as hardware manufacturing, transportation/shipping, and landfill/renewable disposal were more often than not missing from the carbon emissions data for each category. The visualization provides information about a snapshot in time, but fails to account for past and future emissions.

## Artist Statement
The environmental cost that concerns me the most isn’t any one category, rather, the scale at which this is occurring. Using my visualization as an example, it takes 121 million ChatGPT queries to equal the amount of carbon emissions for the training of the AI model. Do you know how many queries ChatGPT receives on a typical day? 2.5 billion. That already means that in a single day, ChatGPT queries alone generate 21 times the amount of carbon emissions than the initial training. All of the other categories shown are, again, snapshots in time. The reality is that these levels of carbon emissions are happening every day, multiplied exponentially by each user on the planet. These emissions warm the planet, recede the coasts, and so much more. That is what concerns me.

I truly do not believe that AI can be sustainable. Thinking realistically, nothing is truly sustainable (perhaps there are a few things, but you understand my point). But in particular, AI is driven by consumption, and consumption is enabled by extraction. Both parts, consumption and extraction, wreak havoc on the environment and drain ever smaller pools of resources. It would take a miracle of a redesign to make AI sustainable: doesn’t use dirty fuel, doesn’t waste clean water, relies on fully recycled materials…the impossible list goes on. I cannot imagine a future where AI is ultimately harmless, but I can imagine a future where AI is better, and used more efficiently for the greater good.

I believe it is my responsibility to exercise judgement over whether or not I should use AI to help me complete a task. I cannot control built-in AIs in my PC and in websites, but I can choose to do a Google search for movie recommendations over using ChatGPT. I can ask a friend to look over my essay rather than giving it to ChatGPT to review. These small choices are what I have the most control over, and I take full advantage of having those choices. Similarly, I believe I am responsible for making others aware of the impact AI has, now that I am aware of it myself.
