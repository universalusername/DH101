## Design Statement
My game is about the player’s first day on the job as a content moderator. The twist? It’s set in a dystopian future where the government is always watching and AI has the last say about people’s fates– including your own! As the player either ‘confirms’ or ‘false alarms’ AI powered content flags, notes about the player’s action will be taken and the experience will change accordingly. There are a total of 5 endings: compliance (didn’t question the system/obeyed), resistance (refused to confirm most flags), instability (erratic/indecisive behaviour confused the system), early exit (you walked out halfway through to avoid the consequences of your actions) and finally, the secret joke ending: McDonalds (you never started your job and left for a Big Mac). In this game, every choice matters, and every choice is recorded. Just as your performance is evaluated at work and school, the system here is monitoring your performance and responding in turn. Going against the system warrants you warnings and eventually gets you fired. Going with the system still has regular checkpoints but grants you a promotion in the end. Going 50/50 on ‘confirms’ and ‘false alarms’ gets you a much more introspective and self aware ending that ultimately leads to your demise due to your unpredictability. Being able to leave partway through gives the player a sense of responsibility; if you leave, you can shed your guilt, but if you stay, you accept any future consequences. It also makes the gameplay a little less repetitive and gives the illusion of free choice. The rules and choices here highlight the complicity/harm part of content moderation and the detached, cold nature of AI. Your ‘confirms’ lead to people disappearing and dying, but at the same time, too many ‘false alarms’ makes you lose your job or even be killed yourself for being a loose cannon. The speech manner and style of the AI responses reinforce the idea that this is your fault; after all, it is clearly a machine that isn’t influenced by emotion.

## Artist Statement
Games as a form gives the player autonomy (or the illusion of it) in a way that really reinforces the concept of responsibility and blame. By placing the power in the player’s hands, they are forced to come to terms with the fact that yes, it is, in fact, their own actions that lead to a certain outcome. Oftentimes this affects the player much more than a written story does, because of the control they have in contrast. Additionally, games and their rules invoke a certain level of creativity and rule-bending on the player’s side that can draw out unseen conclusions. By thinking outside the box in my game, for example, you can avoid the conflict altogether by simply not playing the game: you can leave without clocking in and go about your day to get McDonalds. Perhaps a silly example, but when I was writing out the beginning, I realized I should have a choice to clock in (begin the game) or not. After all, if I were the player and my only option was to clock in and begin, I’d wonder if my decisions really mattered to begin with.
AI collaboration was very helpful in my case. I didn’t want nor need it for the story itself, as I had already had a fleshed out idea, BUT! I did use it to help me with the Inky syntax and variables, as it is a new program to me and their help page is quite sparse. Additionally, I used AI to determine the length of my story to fit within the 5-10 minute range for the assignment. I found that trying to time myself produced faster times in comparison to my roommate, who was unfamiliar with testing. So, I used AI to give me a guideline that would put me where I needed to be time-wise. Finally, I used AI to do some proofreading and general grammatical/syntactical checks. This was particularly helpful because Inky doesn’t flag misspelled words, and I wrote ~580 lines, which is a lot to check by hand.
I think if I were to do this assignment again, I would go with a different approach to the story altogether. While I like the dystopian, creepy, AI-is-watching-you sort of theme I currently have, I feel like it’s one well explored already (think GLaDOS and HAL 9000). I feel like I’d go with a story about AI being so integrated into daily life that you cannot escape it…or can you?


## Attribution & AI Use
- Tool used: ChatGPT
- Prompt summary: Provide a number of questions for this story [my story] that would have an ~8min playtime. Provide examples of Inky syntax for variables and conditionals. Check if [my story] is correct. Proofread for syntax and grammar errors. [my story].
- Responses: 12 questions. Provided examples and current usage is correct. Everything looks good, try implementing a secret 6th ending about the AI crashing.
- What I kept/changed/discarded and why: I changed the number of questions to 10 because it ended up fitting the time better. The variables were good so I kept them. Grammar/syntax was good so I kept that, but didn’t use the 6th ending idea because it didn’t align with the core of my story. It’s not about the AI, it’s about the player.
