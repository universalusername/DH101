## Network Analysis
The most central node in my network is the consumer. While the consumer is passive and contributes nothing to the actual construction of AI, without the consumer, there would be no network to begin with. Companies focus on profits, and rarely (if ever) do things just for the sake of ‘doing things’; where there is demand, companies will rise to meet it, creating networks such as the one I depict. When demand falls, companies move on to the next desirable thing, chasing profits and leaving behind empty warehouses, laid off workers, and destroyed environments. That is why the consumer is most central: their whims control the network itself.
The most prevalent groups are in the countries across the network. Lesser developed countries appear most often on the extraction/distribution end of the network. They are the ones doing the most grunt work and feeling the harmful environmental effects of AI firsthand. More developed countries, on the other hand, appear most often on the other end of the network: manufacturing, tech companies, and data centers. Their greater wealth allows them to use the raw materials from lesser developed countries to build more valuable products. After production, companies based in these countries are able to use the products to service their consumer base, most of which are in the very same more developed countries. The inequality is clear: lesser developed countries are exploited, while more developed countries enjoy the benefits.
The most surprising part for me was the lack of tech company representation in the manufacturing cycle. There are a few recognizable names: Intel, AMD, NVIDIA, and Samsung. However, I expected big tech company players to have a bigger presence in the manufacturing cycle, like Apple, Meta, and Microsoft. I would have thought that they design and create their own specialized parts for their products, but I suppose when your company is that big, outsourcing isn’t much of an issue; the name of the company is enough for consumers to believe in a product’s quality, not that it is ‘handcrafted’ or of original design.
The biggest pieces missing from my network are environmental impact and human labor. Every part of the cycle has a negative effect on the environment: material extraction pollutes local environments and destroys natural habitats, distribution pollutes the air, land, and sea through shipping vehicle emissions (planes, trucks, and boats), manufacturing produces harmful byproducts that also pollute local environments, data centers consume too much water and electricity while polluting the air, and tech companies and consumers enable and perpetuate these effects. As for human labor, think of the miners, drivers, factory workers, content moderators, and everyone else that does the hard work so that tech CEOs and consumers can kick back and enjoy the final product. The workers are most directly affected by the environmental impact of AI, and yet they often never get a piece of the completed product.


## Artist Statement
As I’ve stated before, power clearly lies with northern developed countries, as it has for much of recorded history. Southern, lesser developed countries are stripped of their resources for cheap and left with salted earth and broken populations. Again, AI as an industry is just a new tool to reinforce neocolonialism, and after AI there will come another tool until there is nothing left to exploit. The ‘visible’ people in the network are large, faceless corporations and consumer base. Companies that mine, distribute, manufacture, and sell to the masses are clearly shown, but are not humanized. It is difficult to rally for change when the opponent in question is a megacorporation; even having a face to go with the name, like Jeff Bezos to Amazon, you know that he is so far removed from daily proceedings and so wealthy that any complaint is easily brushed off. Therefore, in a network full of these faceless megacorporations, it feels more than impossible to change the tides of how things are currently, from harmful to the masses and to beneficial for the few, to beneficial for all and harmful to none.
Additionally, the most invisible people in this network are the ones physically keeping it going: the hands-on laborers. Those who directly build the chips, that mine the copper, moderate the data, and keep the data centers running smoothly. Those everyday workers make up the force that is both most affected and least helped by what they are creating. They often suffer in silence, as they have little access to spread their experiences and raise their voice. Even if they do, those with more power, high ranking tech officials, work hard to make sure their message is lost and discouraged.
A different network structure might change the story by influencing where you think power appears to be. If corporations were central instead of consumers, the network would show that companies more often shape demand rather than simply respond to it. A labor-centered network would emphasize the workers who are most affected but least visible, and draw attention to the relatively indolent and negligent higher ups (CEOs, directors, other executives). An environmental structure would make ecological damage the focus, highlighting the disparity once more between the exploited and affected to the beneficiaries. Overall, changing the structure would change perspectives of responsibility, power, and central issue.
