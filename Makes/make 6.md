## Reseasrch Documentation
Sources cited below. Unsurprisingly, neither potential data center sites nor mining locations were easy to find. The public will only ever be able to see a company’s manufactured facade: the do-no-wrong exterior that keeps us pacified and perpetuates our consumption of their products. The ugly interioir–child labor, exploitation/neocolonialism, environmental damage–is kept out of sight and out of mind. 
Data center sites are hidden for 2 main reasons: community/political backlash, and to prevent land price spikes. Data centers are notorious for being disruptive and useless for locals; they drive up electricity cost, use communal water at a faster rate than it can be replenished, pollute the air, provide little employment, and generate a lot of noise from their cooling systems. If a community were to find out about a prospective nearby data center, it is very likely that they would organize resistance. Companies get around this publicity through NDAs with local governments. These quiet negotiations and NDAs also work to prevent those land prices spikes; if Google were to announce that they will build a data center at 10 Main Street, then 6, 7, 8, 9, 11, 12, 13, and 14 Main Street would increase land prices to try and force Google’s hand to pay more whenever they inevitably build more data centers in the area.
On the other hand, mining sites are kept quiet for slightly different reasons. Mining operations tend to happen the most in the global south, especially in Bolivia, Argentina, Chile, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo. These areas have historically been taken advantage of by the global north, and these mining operations are no different. Mining has 3 main issues: environmental damages, community displacement, and labor controversies. Environmental damage includes (but is not limited to): Water and air pollution, erosion, biodiversity loss, and climate change. Oftentimes if a community sits on or near a mineral deposit, they will be displaced either by extreme suggestion (essentially forced out) or due to health effects from pollution. Not only that, but for a local to secure a job at one of these mining sites is near impossible–labor is outsourced and brought in. Lastly, labor controversies continue to threaten human rights. Outsourced labor or not, these workers are often paid poorly and are subject to dangerous working conditions often without emergency plans or safety precautions. Child labor is unfortunately also common in this industry, as is the forced employment of those who have been trafficked. With little government enforcement of human rights and environmental policy, mining sites are certainly a sore spot for big tech.
Overall, while none of these conclusions surprised me (I’ve had a chance to look into most of this before), the reminder of what's hidden behind every pin on the map is disheartening. Every data center–a risk to the health of children playing in the park nearby. Every mining site–an unsafe death trap that has claimed countless lives and forced out communities overnight. Each pin has unimaginable human suffering in either or all of their past, present and future. Each headquarters spins a tale of a bright, tech-based future, and yet every cog in their machine argues that we won’t live to see it.

## Artist Statement
My map argues that there is a disparity between those that use AI the most and those who are most negatively affected by its use. By showing data center locations (cheap land areas, global north) and mining sites (countries with more unstable governments, global south), as well as the distances/connections between these locations, the argument is made that the AI industry is reviving (not that it ever went away) colonialism in a new form. Global AI infrastructure (as shown) focuses primarily on extraction of materials from lesser developed countries (global south) to cater to the desires and consumption of users who reside primarily in the global north, reflecting historical power imbalances and resource exploitation under a new term: neocolonialism. 
While there is a point to be made that even those in the global north suffer from the costs associated with AI and the data centers built there (environmental damage, land price hikes, etc.), the global south carries a much heavier burden, as mineral extraction operations pose as horrific human rights violations. They move entire communities, quietly kill countless workers (often due to carelessness; companies can’t be bothered to have an extra expense for proper safety precautions), destroy and contaminate large swathes of land, and take advantage of illegal human trafficking operations and our most vulnerable population, children, for cheap labor.
CEOs try to hide these costs; a map drawn by the CEO of Google would certainly not have the over 50 planned and running data centers that mine does, much less the mining sites, oh no. Bad publicity. A worker in an Argentinian mine, however, would have a different idea. They might show old mining sites, long abandoned, that had once been their hometown. All that is left there are rows upon rows of dried up brine ponds, not a plant or animal in sight. They might show dozens of mining sites not on official record, all across the country; hidden operations that aren’t subject to government regulation.
On the surface, one might say that the biggest things my map is missing are key components of AI infrastructure, such as content moderation and data labelling locations, cable connections, raw material processing and other material mining sites. It is true that these parts aren’t shown, but I disagree that they are the biggest missing pieces. The most glaring absence is that of human suffering. A data center pin won’t tell you outright that it is draining drinking water from the nearby dam, or polluting the air to a dangerous degree. The suffering of the residents–water shortages, asthma/allergy/lung conditions–goes unreported. Mining site pins won’t say how many workers died in a branch collapse, nor that 15% of its labor force is under 13 years old. Neither will mention the name of the town that quietly disappeared after the center was built and the mines were blown, whole communities driven out due to impossible economic and living conditions.
This map only scratches the surface of the web of AI. One can only wonder if the rich will ever stop getting richer, and the exploited ever be able to break free from the cycle. From the looks of it, AI infrastructure will do nothing but repeat the power imbalances of history, no matter the human cost.
